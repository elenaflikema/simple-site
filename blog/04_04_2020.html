The past couple of weeks has brought me back to my days wearing a uniform, bottled up on a submarine for weeks.....or months on end, counting down the days until i could breathe fresh air again. Memories have been flooding my mind, putting a smile on my face, Never had I thought that spending time on submarines would condition my mind for anything in my life post Navy.

April, 2020, Ho Chi Minh City

Day 19- the number of days of my first op. Also, the number of days I've been restricted to my flat, and roof, thank god! I can't say the two situations are similar. Here, I can still go downstairs to the market and buy food, alcohol, snacks, etc. I can also go up to the roof and soak up some rays, do my workout, and take in the view at sunset. Netflix, Youtube, etc(on a sub, we had very limited entertainment, internet was impossible). Also, I'm "stuck" with my wife. I feel like the luckiest person in the world to have the love of my life by side throughout this. There is no one else I would want to go through this with! And last, but not least...I brought my PS4 with me from Russia. It looks like a wise move at this point, a nice getaway from reality.

On a "boat", entertainment consisted of a small selection of films that we would watch in the galley as a group unless you had your own laptop. A library of books that were passed around amongst the crew(I think i read every Tom Clancy novel written up to 2005), or some downloaded games on a personal laptop(back when laptops were big, clunky, and a far comparison from what everyone uses today!) Some boats also were able to fit in a treadmill, an exercise bike, or some free weights. Quite often near the reactor compartment or the smoking area. Doubly beneficial, right? Oh, and hot racking! That is something I don't miss.

What is hot racking I'm sure you are asking yourself......

Simply put, you share "your" rack(meaning bed) with one or two other guys on your team. When I was working an eight-hour shift, my trainee was sleeping in "my" rack. Then another trainee or operator would sleep in it for another eight hours, then back to me again. Working 12s( a 12 hour shift or more) was much more preferable. Then you would only have to share your bed with one other person. The comforts of sub life!

Swim call post-op

Other obvious things missing on a sub-a market, alcohol selection, a sunset, and fresh air. The closest came to a sunset on a boat was staring at a black and white digital display of the view from the periscope. Depressing.......

My first trip on a sub went by quickly. It was quite intense. 18 hour days of training and working. Six hours of sleep or less I'd say. My rack, all of the "riders"(guys temporarily assigned to a platform for specific missions) racks were usually in the torpedo room so sleep wasn't always peaceful. Lights were on, people walking by your bed, torpedomen working, talking. The one constant was the sound of the fuel in the tomahawk missiles swishing back and forth as the submarine rocked gently back and forth below the surface of the water. It should have been more alarming than anything I suppose. After all, it was a gigantic rocket meant to rain down death and destruction upon the enemy.

I made history on that trip, that I'll never forget. At my own expense of course. I can say that my supervisor hadn't been supervising so much up to this monumental moment. I woke up from a deep slumber, and stumbled to the head(sailor word for bathroom). There was some sign on the bathroom stall door which I didn't give much attention to. I was groggy, my vision hazy,my bladder about to burst. I did my duties and zipped up my overalls. I reached for the lever coated in red paint to flush the toilet and pulled it. This strange noise bellowed out from the depths of the metallic beast as I opened "the hatch". It was like someone gargling, but mechanical, pressurized. All of a sudden, liquid, organic waste, bodily fluid of every type spewed out of this toilet and covered me completely. I had no idea what had just happened! Some guys ran in and asked me what I had done. I was so confused. I felt like I had just committed a crime. Covered in piss, poo, and only god knows what else, I felt humiliated!

Come to find out, there is a time when a submarine "blows sanitaries", meaning the sewage is released from the submarine into the ocean. Flushing the toilet during this process can break the plumbing system and cause serious problems(luckily for the boat and I, the only consequence was me having to get hepatitis shots and an iodine shower after)

Word travels faster than the speed of light on a boat! Before I left the toilet, I'm pretty sure word had traveled from the torpedo room to the maintenance bay, galley, all the way to the radio room. After cleaning up I went upstairs to the radio room and reported to my supervisor. No one could believe what happened to me. Shock and awe.. I was the first person in XX years who "blew sans" on himself. For the next three or four days, I ate in the galley alone. The crew kept their distance from me...like "social distancing". Many jokes about it. They wore gloves around me and eventually I was congratulated by the crew for making history.

Before we arrived back to Pearl Harbor, everyone had been told the news about my historic moment. I walked into the shop, jokes galore, in excess. Relentless, endless... I never lived it down. It gave me fame, publicity..err infamy, a new identity. The legend grew, along with a few other tall tales. A new name was soon bestowed upon me

Jimmy the Flick

Why do we use this construction?

-to say that one past action happened and finished before another past action

-he went on his first op after he had spent nine months at AIT

-if we are only discussing one past finished action, we don't use past perfect
