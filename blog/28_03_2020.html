If you haven't read my blog about my gas chamber experience in bootcamp, take a look at that first! I've been wearing a mask in public for the past two weeks or so. It isn't always comfortable, but it is necessary in this era. Many people however, laugh it off, make a mockery of those who wear masks, and take precautions. The mask has become a part of me. The group of people who ridicule those who wear masks are mostly foreigners here who claim that it will not protect you, it is useless. Those foreigners exacerbate the issues we are already dealing with. As expat, we are viewed as a problem, carriers of the virus (as I mentioned in a previous blog). By not wearing a mask, we are solidifying this stereotype and enforcing the discrimination which we are up against.

The idea of the mask is to prevent yourself from transmitting bacterias/viruses to others by coughing and spreading the virus amongst the population. The government has also introduced a new law. Everyone must wear a mask in public places! It is the law now, but many foreigners have decided it's not worth following the law. The arrogance, the ignorance, the foolery of people. We are guests in this country! Respect the local rules and culture. Adapt to where you live as an expat, you can't expect the country to adapt to you. It makes me livid to see people behave in such a way and paranoid about where I live. I reside in a living complex which is saturated with foreigners and some locals mixed in. Americans, Brits, Italians, Africans, Indians, Georgians,etc. You name it, they are probably here. Staying away and in my flat as much as possible!

Ok...now that I've ranted and raved, lets travel back in time to my last days of bootcamp. Our final "test" of bootcamp was called Battle stations. It was something that we, as a unit were working towards from Day 1. Seven weeks of being broken down mentally and physically. RDC's instilling discipline, organization, respect, and attention to detail into our minds. That whole process from beginning to end was intense. P-Days to Battle stations(P-Days) is another story for another day. From how we had to make our beds, locker and uniform inspections, marching. At the time, those "events" seemed so useless, senseless, but come Battle stations....the new me. The me who was no longer timid, afraid of "what's next". This me was someone who was ready to face senseless, take on new challenges, and do whatever had to be done. I was reprogrammed, reborn in some ways.

Battle stations

The day before Battle stations, we were told to rest, sleep, and have our things ready. We had only been sleeping 6 hours max a night up to that point, less if you had a two-hour guard duty(called a watch, 0000-0200 was the worst!) Anyways, it was maybe 1400 and we were told to go to sleep. I don't really remember sleeping. I only remember staring at the bunk above me anticipating what was to come. This moment, the final test was about to arrive! I was restless my mind racing. Trying to mentally prepare for the end of this and graduate from bootcamp. Envisioning how this would all unfold, what I would have to go through, all of the stories I had heard about Battle stations from our instructors and the "holdbacks" All of a sudden, alarms went off! Our instructors barged into our bay and ordered us to put on our uniforms on, don our gas masks, and form up. We were led outside for what would be one of many runs in full kit, gas masks strapped to our domes.

The next 24 hours was a blur. I remember jumping off a high dive in my uniform, making a life jacket out of it, and boarding an emergency raft in the pool, all the while divers pulling at my legs, throwing me off from my task at hand. I recall running at dusk with those heavy-ass boots on, sucking for air, and the stench of my uniform due to the sweat and dirt which had more or less formed a symbiotic relationship with each other over the previous seven weeks(those uniforms were thrown out after bootcamp). The running was brutal. The mask asphyxiating me more and more with every step, at the same time we were singing a cadence. There were times when we were allowed to remove it. I can only think back on one other time in my life when the air felt fresher than it did during this "exercise"(that was after 119 days on a submarine with very little to no fresh air. Those first breaths while docking in Bahrain were glorious!)

Some other hazy memories from that night........carrying a "battle buddy" on a stretcher through some crazy obstacle course in the dark. Barbed wire, razor wire, hoisting him over walls, through tunnels, etc. I'm pretty sure that drill was to focus on our communication skills while being deafened by speakers blaring simulated explosions, alarms, automatic fire, and more.

At last, morning came. Dawn was upon us. The finish line was so close. We all knew it, we felt it, and were feeding of of each others energy and motivation at this point. By this time, we had become automated in our actions. Reflexive, numb to the tiredness, the aches and pains in our legs and knees. Hyper-focused and hungry for victory. We entered a room where some kind of ceremony began taking place. We were all given NAVY hats, discarding the RECRUIT hats which had labeled us for the previous two months. The national anthem started. We all saluted the flag, a few tears rolled down my face. I remember feeling embarrassed, crying in a room full of guys, but I knew we were all feeling that same high. Up to that point in my life, bootcamp was the most challenging event I had gone through. It gave me a new sense of pride, confidence, and respect for myself and my brothers around me. We did it together. Teamwork, hard work, discipline, and accountability. This event set the stage for what was to come in the following years. This was only the beginning of it all.....
