Yesterday I went to the gym for the last time until only god knows how long. The place was a ghost town, but like any decent human being, I took some precautions and wore my mask out of consideration for others.

I hopped on the treadmill after warming up and began my cardio session. About two kilometers into my run, I felt like I was suffocating. My mask was constantly disrupting my breathing pattern and blocking my nasal passages. It was uncomfortable to say the least and started sapping my energy and motivation. Breathing through my mouth was even worse! I nearly swallowed my mask while gasping for air(I gave up on that running idea shortly after and hit the weights).

My grandma and grandpa Flickema and I,
fresh out of boot camp

Running on the treadmill brought back memories of bootcamp. What a time I'll never forget! It has been about nineteen years since I was hurried off of a bus at Great Lakes Training Center in Illinois and thrown into the fire. So many situations, events, experiences during that nine weeks that have been etched into my mind for eternity.

Two vivid and nostalgic moments came to mind yesterday. The first row was getting gassed. The gas chamber. OH.....MY.....GOD! The buildup to that "exercise" was tormenting, frightening, panic attack-inducing! So many questions and not many answers. Every division has that one guy who is set back a week or two and "knows the ropes". I remember this guy spreading legendary s*#! about the gas chamber and most of us bought into what he was saying. The majority of us were fresh out of high school, 18-20 years old I'd say. Fresh meat, naive, and clueless sums us up as a whole pretty well. Our RDCs(meaning instructors) were also sowing seeds of fear and doom into our minds. They were overly effective with that stuff. They must have worked in psy-ops before becoming RDCs


Gas chamber day

We were marched as a division to this room, practiced putting on and securing our masks, making sure there were no leaks, everything was sealed. We filed in to this room. The smell I remember now is so distinct, but impossible to describe. It was sour, pungent, spicy...I don't even know. We were organized into rows and columns about twenty people deep if I recall correctly. We were given directions to put on our masks and await further orders. Once I tightened the mask, fastened the straps, and began breathing, I became a bit panicky. I was in the fourth or fifth row and couldn't really understand what was being said in the front of the room. The guy next to me looked at me and I him. We were in the same position. We couldn't make out what was going on, but we saw people taking their masks off and we followed suit. Next thing you know, the instructor began screaming at my battle buddy and I because we hadn't understood. We hadn't been told to remove our masks. Only the first row was given that instruction. As punishment for our "lack of attention to detail", we were forced to do jumping jacks, pushups, and other physical exercises while "getting used to" the C.S. gas. It probably only lasted 4-5 minutes, but felt endless . For us, it truly lasted forever because we also participated with the second, third, and fourth rows. It was terrible, torturous! I remember my eyes watering, burning, felt like they were on fire. Imagine someone pouring red chili pepper juice into your eyes. My throat felt like it was closing off, constant coughing, and stuff draining out of my nose. It wasn't the usual junk that comes out of your nose when you blow it. It was white, clumpy, and hard as a rock. We were ordered out of the room and to wash up. We made it outside and SANITIZED! I thoroughly scrubbed my hands, washed my face and started to gain control of my breathing again. The coughing had about ceased at this point. That smell though...that smell lingered in the open air and I remember washing my dungarees ten times over in the barracks. The aroma of the gas chamber haunt(verb-to recur or visit again) us all for the remainder of bootcamp and still, to this day I will never forget.


Battle stations is on my mind now. Check back in a couple of days for part two of this saga,

Josh


Take note of the underlined words above

-only the first row was given that instruction

-this is an example of passive voice in past simple tense. We modify the form of to be to designate what tense we are using and always follow up with the third form of the verb

to be+3rd form is the base construction for passive voice in simple tenses


past simple=was/were+3rd form

present perfect=have/has+been+3rd form

-rather than using a general subject+verb statement (active voice), we use object+to be+3rd form to emphasize the action over the subject

-if the action is more important than the subject, use passive voice

-if you subject is more important than the action, use active voice
